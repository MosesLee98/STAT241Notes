Disjoint/Mutually Exclusive Events

  • Disjoint - events have no common outcomes
  • P(A and B) = 0
  • Example: A is even numbers, B is odd numbers

Union of Two Events

  • Union of A and B consists of outcomes in (A or B) and (A and B)
  • Denoted P(A or B)

Approaches to Assigning Probabilities

  • Classical Approach - assumption of equally likely outcomes
  • Relative Frequency - experimentation or historical data
  • Subjective Approach - assignor's judgement

Example:


If 85% Canadians like either hockey or baseball, 65% like baseball and 45% like hockey, what is the probability that a randomly chosen Canadian likes baseball and hockey?


  • A: likes baseball, B: likes hockey
  • P(A) = 0.65, P(B) = 0.45, P(A or B) = 0.85
  • Find P(A and B)

Conditional Probability

  • Used to determine how 2 events are related - determine probability of 1 event given the occurrence of another related event
  • For any 2 events, A and B with P(B) > 0, the conditional probability of A given B has occurred written as P(A|B)

Multiplication Rule:

Independent Events Defined Using Conditional Probabilities

  • 2 events A and B are independent if knowing that one occurs does not change the probability that the other occurs
  • P(A|B) = P(A)
  • Idependent iff P(A and B) = P(A)P(B)
  • Probability that 2 independent events will occur = product of the 2 independent probabilities
